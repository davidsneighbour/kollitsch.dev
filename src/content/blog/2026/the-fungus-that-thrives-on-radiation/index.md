The Fungus That Thrives on Radiation—From Chernobyl to Space
In the ruins of Chernobyl’s reactor, scientists made a startling discovery: a mysterious black fungus quietly growing on the walls and even in pools of radioactive water. Far from being harmed by the intense radiation, this fungus seemed to enjoy it—it was not just surviving, but actively spreading towards the most irradiated spots[1]. The organism, later identified as Cladosporium sphaerospermum, appeared to be “feeding” on the deadly gamma rays, almost as if it were converting the radioactive fallout into food. This curious behavior earned it and similar species the name radiotrophic fungi—fungi that use radiation as an energy source[2].
How can a living thing eat radiation? The secret lies in a pigment these fungi have in abundance: melanin. Melanin is the same pigment that gives human skin its color, but in these fungi it serves a unique purpose. In the high-radiation environment of Chernobyl, C. sphaerospermum and other black molds were found to have melanin in their cell walls that absorbs gamma radiation and somehow converts it into chemical energy for growth[3]. In essence, the fungus is performing a kind of pseudo-photosynthesis—using a different part of the electromagnetic spectrum. Instead of sunlight, it harnesses ionizing radiation in a process scientists have dubbed “radiosynthesis”[2][4]. This is analogous to how plants use sunlight, and indeed researchers have noted that the fungus’s melanin-based trick is functionally similar to plants using chlorophyll, just with gamma rays instead of visible light[5]. The result is that these fungi can literally thrive on radiation: experiments confirmed that melanin-rich fungi grow faster in high-radiation conditions than they do in normal background radiation[6]. Take away or block their melanin, and they lose that advantage—highlighting how vital the pigment is to their radiation-fed lifestyle.
The discovery of this radiation-loving fungus in the Chernobyl exclusion zone opened up a world of questions and possibilities. If a fungus can not only withstand extreme radiation but actually use it, could it help us deal with radiation in other ways? Scientists became intrigued by the idea that radiotrophic fungi might be useful allies for managing radioactive environments[7]. For instance, could they assist in bioremediation at sites like Chernobyl, by locking up or “soaking” radiation that would otherwise be hazardous[7]? It’s still an open question how much direct cleanup help a fungus can provide, but the mere fact that nature produced a radiation-consuming organism is fascinating. It shows how life can adapt to even the most hostile of niches—offering a hint of “healing” in a place thought irreversibly scarred by nuclear disaster.
Perhaps the most exciting prospect to emerge from this research is in the realm of space travel. If these fungi can live off radiation, they might also shield other living things from it. Space is filled with intense cosmic rays and solar radiation that pose one of the biggest dangers to astronauts. Engineers usually think of heavy physical shields (like lead or thick water tanks) to block space radiation, but here is a biological alternative: a living, self-regenerating radiation shield. In 2018, a team of researchers sent C. sphaerospermum to the International Space Station to test this idea[8]. They grew a sample of the fungus in a Petri dish aboard the ISS for 30 days, with radiation sensors measuring how much cosmic radiation got through. The results were modest but promising: once the fungal colony had matured into a thin layer (about 1.7 millimeters thick), it reduced the radiation levels behind it by roughly 2% compared to an unprotected control[9]. That may sound small, but it was achieved with just a thin layer of material. The team noted that the thicker the fungal layer, the more radiation it blocked—a straightforward correlation[9]. Extrapolating from the ISS data, they estimated that about a 21 cm thick coating of this fungus could cut the annual radiation exposure on the surface of Mars significantly[10]. In other words, a slab of living fungus about 21 cm thick might absorb a meaningful chunk of the harmful cosmic rays that would otherwise hit a Mars astronaut over a year’s time.
Equally intriguing was how the fungus behaved in orbit. It turned out that C. sphaerospermum not only survived the space radiation—it actually grew faster in it. The ISS experiment noted about a 21% growth boost for the fungus in the space station’s radiation environment[9]. It’s as if being exposed to cosmic rays made the fungus flourish even more, just as it did under Chernobyl’s gamma rays. This further supports the idea that the fungus’s radiosynthesis trick works beyond Earth. A living shield that strengthens as it absorbs radiation is a pretty appealing concept for deep-space missions.
Using a fungus as a radiation shield does sound like science fiction, and there are plenty of hurdles to consider. But there are some practical advantages that have scientists paying attention. For one, a fungal shield could grow itself—astronauts might take a small stock of spores or cultures and breed the protective material on-site (whether in orbit or on another planet), avoiding the need to launch massive heavy shielding from Earth. The fungus can also repair itself if damaged, as any living colony would try to regrow, making it a self-healing barrier. C. sphaerospermum in particular is known to be extremely hardy: it tolerates cold temperatures, high salt, dryness, and even highly acidic conditions that would kill many other organisms[11]. Those qualities are useful when you’re dealing with the harsh conditions of space or a planetary surface. Researchers have even speculated about mixing this fungus or its melanin into building materials—imagine Martian habitats with walls infused with radiation-eating fungi, or astronauts’ suits coated in melanin. In fact, there are early ideas about melanin-based space suits and paints, inspired by these fungi, to help absorb UV and cosmic rays[12] [13]. More fanciful concepts suggest a “live” radiation shield that could line a spacecraft or habitat, or even using radiotrophic fungi to protect and enhance other life support systems [for example, shielding crops grown in space, or producing nutrients][14] [15]. It’s a testament to how the fungus’s adaptation is spurring creative thinking in aerospace science.
Amid all the excitement, though, scientists are careful not to overhype what is still a nascent field. There are important unknowns and precautions if we ever plan to bring radiotrophic fungi into closed human environments like a space station or Mars base. One obvious concern is biological safety. After all, C. sphaerospermum is a mold. Is it safe to have it growing where people live and breathe? Fortunately, this particular species isn’t known to be dangerous to healthy humans—it’s mainly encountered as a food spoilage organism or an allergen, and it very rarely causes infections in people[16]. It doesn’t produce any major toxins that we know of[17]. That said, even a harmless mold can become a nuisance in a tightly sealed habitat. Fungal spores could irritate crew members or trigger allergies, and an overzealous colony might clog air filters or spread into places it shouldn’t. NASA already battles unintended microbial growth (including common black molds) on the ISS, so any intentional use of a fungus would need strict controls. Engineers would likely need to contain the fungus within layers or bioreactors so that only its protective properties are shared—and not its spores. There’s also the ecological angle: introducing a non-native fungus to a new world or a closed ecosystem could have unpredictable effects. We’d want to be sure, for instance, that a radiation-fed fungus doesn’t out-compete other microbes in a life support system or develop new quirks in low gravity or high radiation over the long term. So far, no show-stopping risks have emerged, but long-term studies are sparse. This is cutting-edge research, and it’s happening in real time.
Scientists often admit that we still don’t fully understand how the fungus actually turns gamma rays into growth. The broad strokes are known (melanin absorbs energy, something about electron transfer increases), but the detailed biochemistry—the “engine” of radiosynthesis—remains somewhat mysterious[18]. As one researcher put it, this discovery is a tantalizing glimpse at a completely new survival strategy, but for now it remains poorly understood[19]. Ongoing research aims to pin down the mechanism and figure out if we can harness it practically. The curiosity around radiotrophic fungi is driving interdisciplinary efforts: biologists, mycologists, radiation experts, even astrobiologists are teaming up to explore what these little black fungi can do.
In the end, the story of Chernobyl’s radiation-loving fungus is a reminder that life is amazingly resourceful. A catastrophic nuclear accident created an environment of extreme radiation, and instead of being a dead zone for all life, it became a niche for a few adaptable organisms to shine. Now those very organisms might teach us how to better protect ourselves in high radiation environments, whether it’s a damaged reactor site or the surface of another planet. It’s a smooth blend of natural survival and human innovation: a humble fungus using melanin to drink up radiation could one day help shield human explorers on our voyages beyond Earth[3][14]. There’s plenty we don’t know yet, and it’s important not to get ahead of the science—but with each experiment, we learn a bit more about this quirky partnership between fungi and radiation. It’s a development that feels very of this world (given it evolved in places like Chernobyl), yet at the same time it opens our minds to what life might do on other worlds. As researchers often note, nature has had billions of years to invent clever solutions; our job is to pay attention and learn from them, sometimes in the most unexpected of places. And a fungus that turns radiation into life is about as unexpected—and intriguing—as it gets.
